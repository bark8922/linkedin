# Idea Bank

Concrete starting points, grouped by territory. None of these are finished posts. Each is a real thing you've done or seen, with an angle and a note on why it works. The job when you post is to pick one, add the specific detail only you know, and tell it straight. Pull the voice rules from the strategy doc before drafting.

Rough difficulty/comfort tags: **[easy]** low exposure, safe to start with. **[meaty]** more substance, better reach, needs more care.

---

## Territory 1: AI and automation builds (your strongest)

1. **The dashboard that deleted a $6K/month line item.** [meaty]
   You built a financial dashboard that replaced Power BI + a data pipeline + a monthly analyst spend. The angle isn't "look what I saved." It's the weird feeling of a tool you made quietly removing a cost, and what that says about where this is all going. Include the part you were unsure about. Strong candidate for your first long-form/PDF piece.

2. **What actually broke the first time.** [easy]
   Everyone posts the win. Post the version where the first build was wrong: the numbers didn't reconcile, a workflow silently failed, you trusted an output you shouldn't have. "Here's the bug that taught me not to trust my own dashboard for a week." Vulnerable, specific, useful.

3. **I'm not a developer and I built this anyway.** [meaty]
   The honest account of shipping real internal tooling without a traditional engineering background. Not "anyone can code now!" (cringe). More: here's what was genuinely hard, here's what AI actually made possible, here's where I still hit a wall.

4. **The €96K/year number, and why the number isn't the point.** [meaty]
   The recruiting dashboard replaced a chunk of external tooling. The interesting angle: the savings got attention, but the real change was that the team could finally see the pipeline daily instead of waiting on a report. Speed of access > cost saved.

5. **A screenshot and one sentence.** [easy]
   Literally a screenshot of something you built, plus one honest sentence about what it does and one about what you'd change. Low effort, high signal, very on-brand for "I build things."

6. **The n8n workflow I'm slightly afraid of.** [easy]
   You run a fleet of automations. Post about the one that does something important unattended and the small anxiety of trusting it. Relatable to anyone automating real work.

7. **What "AI at work" actually looks like for me day to day.** [meaty]
   Cuts against the hype. Not agents replacing everyone. Just: here are the three unglamorous things I actually use it for, and the two things everyone claims it does that it doesn't do well yet for me.

8. **The thing I automated that I shouldn't have.** [easy]
   A time automating something cost more than doing it by hand, or removed a human judgment that mattered. Counterintuitive, honest, and it signals you have taste, not just enthusiasm.

## Territory 2: The EA-to-operations evolution

9. **The job title says one thing. The work says another.** [meaty]
   You're officially an executive assistant. In practice you build data infrastructure and automation. Post about that gap, without complaint, as an observation about how the role is quietly changing. Many people are living this and no one's naming it.

10. **What I stopped doing when I started building.** [easy]
    The tasks that used to eat your week that now run themselves, and what you do with the time instead. Concrete before/after.

11. **The most useful skill for an EA in 2026 isn't calendar management.** [meaty]
    Careful with the framing so it's not preachy: make it "the skill that's changed my job the most," reported from your own experience, not prescribed to others.

12. **Being the person who connects the tools.** [easy]
    A lot of ops value is just wiring System A to System B so humans stop copy-pasting. Post about one specific integration you built and the boring problem it killed.

13. **I sit next to the work, so I see what's actually broken.** [easy]
    The EA/ops seat gives you a view of where time really leaks in an org. Share one pattern you keep noticing, framed as your vantage point, not a universal law.

## Territory 3: Recruiting from the inside (report, never prescribe)

14. **What I notice about hiring from the ops seat, not the recruiter seat.** [meaty]
    Explicitly frame it: "I'm not a recruiter, but I sit inside a recruiting company and build the systems that track the pipeline, so I see the data." Then one honest observation. The framing is what keeps it credible and humble.

15. **The metric everyone watches vs. the one that actually predicts things.** [meaty]
    From building the recruiting dashboard, you've seen which numbers people stare at and which ones actually move outcomes. Report what the data showed you, carefully.

16. **Time-to-hire is a story about where things get stuck.** [easy]
    You built the time-in-stage views. Share the least obvious place candidates actually get stuck, as a data observation.

17. **What building the funnel taught me about the funnel.** [easy]
    Making the dashboard forced you to define every stage precisely, and that exercise revealed messiness nobody had noticed. The act of measuring changed the understanding.

## Cross-cutting / evergreen

18. **The unglamorous truth about "data-driven."** [easy]
    Being data-driven mostly means someone spent a week cleaning a spreadsheet nobody wants to talk about. Honest, funny, true.

19. **A tool I tried and dropped.** [easy]
    You test a lot of tools. Post about one you expected to love and abandoned, and why. Low stakes, builds trust, invites good comments.

20. **The question I can't answer yet.** [meaty]
    Something you're genuinely wrestling with (e.g. how much to trust an automated output, where the human has to stay in the loop). End unresolved. This is the most anti-cringe format there is: you posting a real open question instead of a fake answer.

21. **One month of building, in five screenshots.** [meaty]
    A PDF carousel: five things you shipped, each with a one-line honest caption. Shows range without a word of bragging.

22. **Why I think most AI-at-work posts are wrong.** [meaty]
    A contradiction-interrupt (state the opposite of what people expect from someone who builds AI tooling). Use sparingly and only when you actually believe it. Grounded in your real experience, this lands; ungrounded, it's just another hot take.

---

## How to turn any of these into a post (quick template)

- **Line 1:** the specific moment or fact. A number, a date, a thing that happened. No windup.
- **Middle:** what you did, what surprised you, what didn't work. Keep it to one idea.
- **End:** the limited, honest thing you took from it, or a real question. No grand claim.
- **Visual:** a screenshot of the actual thing beats any stock image.
- **Then run the self-check:** Could only I have written this? Am I reporting or preaching? Would I cringe if a smart friend read it?
