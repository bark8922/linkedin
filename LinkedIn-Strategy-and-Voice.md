# Blake's LinkedIn Playbook

The whole point: show up as someone who actually builds things, without ever sounding like the LinkedIn people you can't stand. Visibility for you and for Tribe, earned by being specific and real, not by posturing.

---

## The positioning

You are not a recruiter and you should never pretend to be one. You are the person inside a recruiting company who quietly replaced expensive tooling with things you built yourself, and who is figuring out what the operations/EA role becomes when you point AI at it. That is a rarer and more interesting seat than "recruiter with opinions about recruiting."

Three territories you're drawing from:

1. **AI and automation builds** — the actual things. Replacing Power BI + a data pipeline + a $6K/month analyst with dashboards you built. n8n workflow fleets. Agents doing real ops work. This is your strongest material because almost nobody posting about AI has shipped anything; you have.
2. **The EA-to-operations evolution** — what the assistant role turns into when the person in it can build. This is a live, under-discussed shift and you're living it.
3. **Recruiting from the inside** — observations about hiring and talent from the ops seat at a recruiting company. Not "here's how to recruit." More "here's what I notice from where I sit."

You are explicitly *not* doing "learning in public" as a headline identity, but sharing what you learned from a specific thing you did is still fair game and often your best angle.

## Cadence

**One post per week.** Sustainable, and enough to build presence without it becoming a second job. Better to miss a week than to post filler. The daily idea generator exists so that when your posting slot comes up, you already have three or four things worth saying and you pick the best one.

A realistic weekly rhythm:
- The daily push feeds you ideas all week (2 minutes to skim each morning).
- Pick one on, say, Sunday or Monday.
- Draft it in your voice (I can help), sit on it a few hours, post.

## Formats worth using (2026)

Ranked by fit for you:

- **Plain text + one strong image or screenshot.** Still the best format for a personal story or a "here's a thing I built" post. A screenshot of a dashboard you made stops the scroll better than any stock graphic.
- **Native PDF carousel** (swipeable document). Currently the highest-engagement format on the platform. Great for a "how I replaced X" walkthrough, once a month. You have the raw material for these already.
- **Short face-to-camera video**, unpolished, if you ever feel like it. Raw beats produced. Optional, only if it feels natural.
- **Long-form once a month** (article or PDF) gets 5-10x the reach of a normal post. Your "replaced a $6K/mo analyst" story is a natural one of these.

Avoid: highly produced video, motivational graphics, "agree?" engagement bait, anything that looks like it came from a template.

## The voice guide (the anti-cringe rules)

You said most LinkedIn posts are cringe and BS, and you never want to project or sound like you know better than the reader. That instinct is correct and it's also, conveniently, what actually performs in 2026. The algorithm and the audience now reward the opposite of the cringe stuff. So the rules below are both a taste filter and a growth strategy.

### Rules

**1. Lead with a specific thing that happened, not a lesson.**
Cringe posts open with the moral ("Here's what 10 years taught me about leadership"). Good posts open with the moment ("A dashboard I built quietly deleted a $6K/month line item, and I wasn't sure how to feel about it") and let the reader draw the lesson. Specificity is the single biggest anti-cringe lever. "Last month" beats "recently." A real number beats "a lot."

**2. Report, don't preach.**
Write from "here's what I did and what I noticed," never "here's what you should do." The difference between "You need to automate your reporting" (cringe, presumptuous) and "I automated our reporting and here's what surprised me" (real). You're a narrator of your own experience, not an authority handing down rules.

**3. Include the cost, the doubt, or the part that didn't work.**
The thing that separates a real story from a humblebrag is friction. What was annoying, what you got wrong first, what you're still unsure about. "I'm genuinely not sure this was the right call" is a stronger line than any confident conclusion. This also protects you from sounding arrogant, which is your core fear.

**4. No conclusions you haven't earned.**
Don't end on a grand universal claim. End on the actual, limited thing you learned, or an open question. It's fine, often better, to end unresolved.

**5. Write like you talk.**
Short sentences. Contractions. No "thrilled to announce," no "humbled to share," no "let's unpack this." If you wouldn't say it out loud to a coworker, cut it.

**6. One idea per post.**
Not five tips. One thing, told well.

**7. Skip the engagement theatrics.**
No "Thoughts? 👇", no fake vulnerability, no "comment X and I'll send you the template." If the post is good, people respond. If you want a question at the end, make it a genuine one you actually want the answer to.

### The instant-cringe blocklist

Never post: "thrilled/humbled to announce," "I'm excited to share," "hot take," "unpopular opinion" (when it's not), broetry (one. line. per. paragraph. for. drama.), motivational quotes over sunsets, "agree?", fake-modest brags, "Most people won't understand this but," anything that starts by telling the reader what they're doing wrong.

### The self-check before you hit post

Three questions:
- **Could only I have written this?** If a generic "AI thought leader" could have posted it, it's too generic. Add the specific detail that makes it yours.
- **Am I reporting or preaching?** If there's a "you should," rewrite it as "I did."
- **Would I cringe if a smart friend read this?** If yes, it's not ready.

## How the whole system fits together

- **This doc** — your positioning, cadence, and voice. The reference for everything.
- **The idea bank** — a running list of concrete post ideas from your real work, so you're never staring at a blank page.
- **The daily analyzer** — a scheduled task that runs each morning, scans what's actually trending in your world (AI, ops, recruiting/talent, the future-of-work conversation), and hands you 3-5 fresh angles already filtered through the voice rules above. It's an idea feeder, not an autopilot. You always write the real thing (with help), because the whole value is that it sounds like you.
