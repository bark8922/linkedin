# LinkedIn post angles — 2026-07-06 (Monday)

Five angles today: four tied to this week's discussion, one evergreen. Each is a starting point, not a finished post. Filtered through the voice rules: lead with a specific thing, report don't preach, include the friction, end honest.

## 1. The "AI can't replace my EA" headline, from the other side of the desk
**Territory:** EA-to-operations evolution
**Hook:** "A CEO said this week that AI can't replace her executive assistant. I'm an executive assistant who spent last quarter replacing a $6K/month data analyst with AI. Both things are true and it took me a while to see why."
**Direction:** Fortune ran a piece where a CEO who threatened to fire anyone not using AI admitted the tech still couldn't replace her assistant. Blake is living the inverse: the title on his contract is EA, but the work is building systems that replaced external tooling and a contractor. The post reports the split he actually sees: the parts of the job AI ate were the ones that were really just data plumbing; the parts it can't touch are judgment, context, and knowing which number the exec actually needs. Not a defense of the role, just what the line looks like from inside it.
**Why timely:** Fortune, July 5 2026, "The CEO who vowed to 'fire anyone who doesn't use AI' admits the technology can't replace her executive assistant." The debate is live this week.
**Format:** Plain text, no image needed. Maybe one screenshot of a dashboard for proof.

## 2. The 60% governance gap, told as "I am the shadow IT"
**Territory:** AI and automation builds
**Hook:** "The reports this week keep calling it a 'governance gap.' I looked at my n8n instance and realized I am the governance gap."
**Direction:** Multiple 2026 reports flag that ~97% of companies deployed AI agents but a large majority have no formal plan to supervise them, and shadow IT is back as the top consequence. Blake runs a fleet of automations (expense ops, data pipelines) that a lot of the company now quietly depends on and that mostly live in his head. The post reports that honestly: the same speed that makes these builds worth it is exactly what makes them fragile, and the doubt he actually carries is "what breaks the day I'm on holiday." Ends on the open question of where the line between useful and reckless actually sits for a team of one.
**Why timely:** Microsoft 2026 Work Trend Index + WRITER's "79% face challenges" report + CloudApper's "Shadow IT is back, this time it's AI," all circulating this week.
**Format:** Plain text + one screenshot of an n8n workflow canvas.

## 3. "87% of companies use AI in recruiting" — from the data seat, not the recruiter seat
**Territory:** Recruiting observed from the inside
**Hook:** "The stat going around is that 87% of companies now use AI in recruiting, up from 26% two years ago. I'm not a recruiter, but I build the dashboard our recruiters stare at every morning, and the number that actually moved isn't the one you'd guess."
**Direction:** Korn Ferry / MSH / Deloitte 2026 reports are everywhere this week with the human-AI "co-pilot not autopilot" framing. Blake sits at the data layer of a recruiting company: he built the funnel/time-to-hire/recruiter-performance view. The post reports one concrete observation from that vantage point — e.g. where AI actually compressed time in the funnel vs. where it did nothing, or that the bottleneck was never sourcing speed. Reported never prescribed: he's not telling recruiters how to recruit, just what the pipeline data shows from where he sits.
**Why timely:** Korn Ferry TA Trends 2026 ("Human–AI Power Couple") and MSH "87% use AI in recruiting" stat, both current.
**Format:** Plain text + a single cropped funnel chart. Could be a short carousel if the before/after is visual.

## 4. Skills over titles, said by a guy whose title lies about his job
**Territory:** EA-to-operations evolution (crossed with recruiting-from-inside)
**Hook:** "Every 2026 hiring report says the same thing: skills over titles, degrees losing relevance. My title says executive assistant. My last month was DuckDB, MCP connectors, and killing a Snowflake pipeline. Draw your own conclusion."
**Direction:** Skills-based hiring is one of the loudest 2026 recruiting threads. Blake is a walking example of the title/skill gap the reports describe, and he sits inside a recruiting company that hires on exactly this premise. The post reports the tension without moralizing: the org screens candidates for skills over credentials, and meanwhile his own title is the least accurate thing about what he does. Ends on an honest limited take: titles are lagging indicators, and that's fine until the day someone needs to find the person who actually does the thing.
**Why timely:** 2026 skills-based hiring trend (MSH, Metaview, PeopleScout predictions all current this week).
**Format:** Plain text. Strong enough to stand alone.

## 5. EVERGREEN — the €96K line item that used to be a login
**Territory:** AI and automation builds
**Hook:** "The recruiting dashboard I built replaced about €96K a year of external tooling. The part nobody warned me about: once it existed, people trusted it more than the thing that cost €96K."
**Direction:** Doesn't depend on any news. Blake rebuilt the recruiting analytics stack (Bubble.io extract → DuckDB transforms → JSON → hosted dashboard) to replace a Power BI + Keboola/Snowflake pipeline. The post reports the quiet second-order effect: the cost saving was the headline, but the real change was that a daily-refreshed view people actually opened shifted how the team argued about the pipeline. Includes the friction — the week a transform broke silently and nobody noticed until the numbers looked wrong. Ends on a limited takeaway about trust being the actual deliverable, not the dashboard.
**Format:** Plain text + one screenshot, or a short PDF carousel showing the old stack vs. the new one.
