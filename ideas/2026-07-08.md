## Angle 1 — The "40% of agent projects get cancelled" number, from someone whose agents didn't
**Territory:** AI & automation builds
**Hook:** "Gartner says 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by 2027. I keep reading the reason as 'the models aren't good enough.' That's not why mine almost died."
**Direction:** Report on the n8n expense-ops and data-pipeline workflows. The hard part was never the model, it was the boring stuff around it: a schema that changed underneath me, an API rate limit I didn't plan for, the week a pipeline silently wrote nothing and nobody noticed. Say plainly which one bit you and how you found out. Land on the limited takeaway: the projects that survive aren't the smartest ones, they're the ones where someone owns the plumbing.
**Timely because:** Forbes ran "Why 40% Of Agentic AI Projects May Be Canceled By 2027" on July 7, 2026, citing Gartner's finding that cancellations come from management/governance, not model capability.
**Format:** Plain text + one screenshot of the n8n canvas (the messy real one, not a clean diagram).

## Angle 2 — "97% deployed an agent, 23% see ROI" — what the gap actually looks like from inside
**Territory:** AI & automation builds
**Hook:** "97% of companies deployed an AI agent last year. 23% got measurable ROI from it. I only know why because I've been on both sides of that number in the same building."
**Direction:** Contrast one build that landed (the financial dashboard that replaced Power BI + a Keboola/Snowflake pipeline + a ~$6K/month external analyst) with one experiment that quietly didn't earn its keep. Be specific about what made the dashboard stick: it wasn't the tech, it was that people opened it every morning. Don't claim a universal rule; just report what the difference was in the two cases you actually ran.
**Timely because:** Microsoft's 2026 Work Trend Index (agents up 15x YoY) plus KPMG/Writer reporting that only 23% of orgs see ROI from agents — the "adoption vs. value" gap is the story of the year.
**Format:** Plain text + before/after screenshot (old Power BI tab vs. the dashboard), or a short screen recording.

## Angle 3 — Skills-first hiring, watched from the data seat (report, don't prescribe)
**Territory:** Recruiting from the inside
**Hook:** "Everyone's writing that hiring is going skills-first in 2026. I'm not a recruiter, but I build the funnel dashboards, and here's the one number that changed when we looked at skills instead of resumes."
**Direction:** From the recruiting analytics dashboard (the one that replaced external tooling, ~€96K/year), pull one concrete observation about the funnel — e.g. where candidates drop, or how time-to-hire moved for a role defined by skills vs. title. Report it as "here's what I saw in our data," explicitly not "here's how you should hire." Note the caveat: one company, one dataset, could be noise.
**Timely because:** Korn Ferry, Deloitte and Metaview 2026 TA-trends pieces all lead with skills-first hiring and the "human-AI power couple." Blake's edge is being the ops person who actually has the numbers behind the trend.
**Format:** PDF carousel — one chart per slide, plain captions.

## Angle 4 — The EA-who-builds, without the LinkedIn halo
**Territory:** EA-to-operations evolution
**Hook:** "My job title says executive assistant. This week I spent it debugging a DuckDB transform and rebuilding a data pipeline. Nobody changed my title. I just stopped waiting for permission to build things."
**Direction:** Report the actual shift, not a manifesto. What the role becomes when the assistant can build: you stop forwarding the report and start owning the thing that generates it. Include the friction — the parts that are genuinely still assistant work, and the awkwardness of a title that no longer describes the work. End on an honest open question, not a victory lap: is "EA" even the right word for this anymore, or does the label still buy you something useful?
**Timely because:** A wave of Jan–2026 pieces (Zapier, Lewis College, mean.ceo) are all arguing the EA role is becoming "architect of leverage" / systems-builder. Blake is a live example rather than a think-piece.
**Format:** Plain text, no image. Let it be a person talking.

## Angle 5 (EVERGREEN — not news-dependent) — The $6K/month you can delete
**Territory:** AI & automation builds
**Hook:** "For about a year we paid ~$6K a month for an external data analyst and a Keboola/Snowflake pipeline to produce one dashboard. I replaced it with a repo and a build script. Here's the part that still makes me nervous."
**Direction:** Walk through the swap concretely — what the old stack did, what replaced it (GitHub, Cloudflare Pages, DuckDB, a rebuild script), and roughly what it saves. Then include the friction that keeps it honest: now there's no vendor to call when it breaks at 7am, it's just you. Report the tradeoff, don't sell the win. Takeaway stays small: cheaper isn't free, it moves the cost from a bill to your own on-call.
**Timely because:** Evergreen — always usable. Bonus tie-in whenever cost-of-AI / ROI discourse is hot (which is right now).
**Format:** Plain text + a single number graphic (cost before vs. after), or the actual commit history screenshot.
