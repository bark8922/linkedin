# LinkedIn post angles — 2026-07-03

Five angles today. Four tied to this week's threads, one evergreen. All filtered through the voice rules: report, don't preach, lead with a specific thing, include the friction.

---

## 1. The "prove it" bar for AI at work

**Territory:** AI & automation builds

**Hook:** "The new question about AI at work isn't 'are you using it' — it's 'show me one workflow you redesigned around it and prove it paid off.' I have exactly one I can point to, and it took longer than I'd admit."

**Direction:** Report on the financial dashboard that replaced Power BI + a Keboola/Snowflake pipeline + a ~$6K/month external analyst. Don't sell it as a win story — lead with the specific old setup and its cost, then the part that was hard: the first rebuild had numbers that didn't reconcile, and I didn't trust it for weeks. The point is that "redesigned a workflow and can prove it" is a much higher bar than "we use AI," and most of the work is the boring reconciliation, not the AI.

**Why timely:** WRITER's 2026 report says 79% of orgs face AI adoption challenges (up double digits from 2025) and fewer than 40% have scaled beyond pilot; the framing going around this week is "have we redesigned a workflow around AI and can we prove it." (writer.com, Microsoft 2026 Work Trend Index)

**Format:** Plain text + one screenshot of the dashboard (numbers blurred).

## 2. MCP from the seat of someone actually wiring it up

**Territory:** AI & automation builds

**Hook:** "Model Context Protocol is 'suddenly on every executive agenda' this week. I've been quietly wiring connectors together with it for months and the honest version is less exciting: half the work is figuring out which tool returns data in the shape you expected."

**Direction:** Report what it's actually like to use MCP connectors day to day — connecting Ashby, BambooHR, GitHub, Slack, Cloudflare into one place so an agent can pull real data. The friction: the wrapper renames params, a connector returns empty on the first try, you retry with different args. It's powerful, but the executive-deck version skips the plumbing. One honest takeaway, no grand claim about the future of software.

**Why timely:** CIO ran "Why Model Context Protocol is suddenly on every executive agenda" and WorkOS/The New Stack both published 2026 MCP roadmap pieces this cycle; a new stateless-protocol release candidate is dated late July 2026. (cio.com, workos.com, thenewstack.io)

**Format:** Plain text, maybe a short screen recording of an agent pulling live data across two tools.

## 3. What the assistant title becomes when the person can build

**Territory:** EA-to-operations evolution

**Hook:** "My title says executive assistant. Last month I spent most of it deleting a $6K/month line item by building the thing it paid for. I still don't have a clean answer for what to call that."

**Direction:** Report the actual gap between the title and the work, without claiming it's a trend or that anyone should follow it. The specific: what an EA role turns into when you can build systems instead of just running them. Include the awkward part — it's genuinely unclear where the role ends, and "system manager" sounds tidier than it feels. End on the open question, not a manifesto about the future of admin work.

**Why timely:** A wave of 2026 pieces (Zapier, Lucy Walker, mean.ceo) argue the EA role is splitting into "system manager / office ops lead" with a 15–20% pay premium for admins with AI skills. Blake is a live example, so he can report the reality against the trend piece. (zapier.com, anywheretalent.com)

**Format:** Plain text, no image. Personal, low-key.

## 4. What the funnel data actually shows about AI in hiring

**Territory:** Recruiting observed from the inside

**Hook:** "I'm not a recruiter. I'm the person who sees the pipeline as numbers. And from that seat, the thing everyone's worried about — AI in candidate screening — looks different than the headlines say."

**Direction:** Report, strictly from the data/ops seat, one specific observation from the recruiting analytics dashboard — e.g. where time actually goes in the funnel, or how a stage conversion looks — and tie it to the current fraud/trust debate without prescribing anything to recruiters. Be explicit that this is one company's data and I only see the numbers, not the candidate conversations. No advice to recruiters. Just "here's what the data on my screen shows, make of it what you will."

**Why timely:** This week's TA reports are dominated by candidate authenticity (91% of recruiters have spotted deception, deepfake interviews rising) and trust (only 26% of applicants trust AI to evaluate them fairly). Korn Ferry, MSH, and Gartner all published 2026 TA-trend pieces. (kornferry.com, talentmsh.com, gartner.com)

**Format:** Plain text + one clean chart from the dashboard.

## 5. Evergreen — the €96K/year rebuild, told through the doubt

**Territory:** AI & automation builds (news-independent)

**Hook:** "The recruiting dashboard I built replaces about €96K/year of external tooling. The week before we cut over, I was quietly terrified the numbers were wrong."

**Direction:** Evergreen, always usable. Report building the recruiting analytics dashboard (Bubble.io extract → DuckDB transforms → JSON → deployed page) that gave the team a daily funnel/time-to-hire/recruiter view. Lead with the number, then spend most of the post on the friction: validating that the rebuilt numbers matched the old system before anyone trusted it, and the fear of being the single person who owns it. End on a limited takeaway about how the scary part of replacing a vendor isn't the build, it's earning trust in the output.

**Format:** Long-form text, or PDF carousel walking one number from raw source to dashboard.
