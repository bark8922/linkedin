## Angle 1 — The pilot worked because someone cleaned the data by hand
**Territory:** AI and automation builds
**Hook:** "Gartner's line this year is that organisations will abandon 60% of AI projects because the data isn't ready. I believe it, because the first version of my dashboard only worked when I was personally cleaning the export."
**Direction:** Report the unglamorous middle. The demo that impressed people ran on a file he had massaged by hand; the moment it had to run every morning against the live Bubble.io API, half of it fell over — nulls, renamed fields, stage names that meant different things to different recruiters. The actual work of the build was not the AI part, it was deciding what a "stage" is and making that decision survive contact with production. End on a limited takeaway: the reason his pilots shipped wasn't a better model, it was that he owned the extraction end of the pipe and could fix the source instead of apologising for it.
**Why now:** Gartner's "through 2026, organisations will abandon 60% of AI projects unsupported by AI-ready data" is being quoted everywhere this month alongside the MIT "95% of pilots deliver zero measurable P&L impact" figure. The consensus explanation shifted from "the model isn't good enough" to "the data was never there".
**Format:** Plain text. Optionally one screenshot of the ugly transform step — the DuckDB SQL that reconciles stage names. The ugliness is the point.
**Friction to keep in:** Name how long the pipeline was quietly hand-held before it was actually automated. Don't tidy that up.

## Angle 2 — The remediation cost nobody puts in the slide
**Territory:** AI and automation builds / EA-to-operations
**Hook:** "The stat going around is that companies who find their data problems after committing the AI budget end up paying another 60-120% of the project cost just to fix the foundations. I replaced a Power BI and Snowflake setup with something I built myself, and I want to be honest about what that swap actually cost."
**Direction:** The savings number (a ~$6K/month analyst, a ~€96K/year tooling line) is real, but it is not free. The cost is that the thing now has an owner, and the owner is him: the pipeline is his to fix at 8am when a source API changes, and the institutional knowledge lives in one head. That is a genuine risk to the company and he should say so plainly rather than sell the win. End on the real open question: at what point does "cheap because I built it" become "expensive because only I can fix it", and how would he even know he'd crossed that line?
**Why now:** The mid-2026 enterprise-AI ROI reporting is full of the hidden-foundation-cost argument (Ellvero, IntuitionLabs, CloudHew, all July 2026). Almost all of it is written from the buyer's side. Nobody is writing the version from the person who actually replaced the vendor.
**Format:** Long-form plain text. This one earns length. No screenshot — a screenshot would make it look like a brag, and the post is the opposite of a brag.
**Friction to keep in:** This IS the friction. Do not resolve it with a reassuring ending.

## Angle 3 — 97% of executives say they deployed agents. 11% run them at scale.
**Territory:** AI and automation builds
**Hook:** "97% of executives say their company deployed AI agents this year. 11% of enterprises actually run them at scale. I run a small fleet of automation workflows and I think I know where the other 86% are stuck."
**Direction:** Not a lecture — a report on what makes one of his n8n workflows survive versus die. The ones that survive have a trigger, a place to write to, and a person who notices when the output is wrong. The ones that die are the ones that were genuinely clever but had no home and no owner, so they rotted. Be careful not to generalise to enterprises he's never seen: frame it as "here's what kills mine, I assume the failure mode scales up but I can't prove that."
**Why now:** Microsoft's 2026 Work Trend Index plus this month's agent-adoption roundups put executive-reported deployment near-universal (97%) while at-scale deployment sits around 11% for enterprises, 2% for SMBs. The gap between the two numbers is the whole post.
**Format:** Plain text + one screenshot of the n8n canvas, including the workflows that are switched off. Show the graveyard, not the trophy case.
**Friction to keep in:** Count his own dead workflows out loud. If the ratio is embarrassing, that's what makes it worth reading.

## Angle 4 — Skills-based hiring is a data problem before it's a philosophy
**Territory:** Recruiting observed from the ops/data seat
**Hook:** "Everyone's writing that resumes are dead and hiring is going skills-based. I'm not a recruiter, I just build the reporting, and from where I sit the blocker isn't belief — it's that 'skills' aren't a field in anyone's system."
**Direction:** Strictly reporting. In the ATS, the things that are cleanly structured are stage, source, date, owner, outcome. The things that aren't structured are exactly the things skills-based hiring wants to measure. So the moment anyone asks for a skills-based view, someone has to invent a taxonomy and then backfill it by hand, which is the part the trend pieces skip. He can show what IS measurable and be honest that the interesting question currently isn't. No prescription for recruiters — he's describing the schema, not the craft.
**Why now:** The mid-2026 recruiting trend coverage (SHRM, MSH, Robert Half, July 2026) is unanimous that skills-based hiring is the headline shift, and equally unanimous that traditional resumes and interviews are becoming unreliable signals. None of it addresses where the skills data is supposed to come from.
**Format:** Plain text + one screenshot of an anonymised field list from the pipeline. Let the schema make the argument.
**Friction to keep in:** Admit he can't currently answer the question either. That's what makes it reporting instead of a dunk.

## Angle 5 — EVERGREEN: the day my number disagreed with everyone else's
**Territory:** AI and automation builds / EA-to-operations (news-independent, always usable)
**Hook:** "The first time my dashboard disagreed with the number the team had been using for months, my instinct was to assume I'd broken something. I hadn't."
**Direction:** One specific reconciliation. A metric he computed came out different from the accepted figure, and the reflex — his and everyone else's — was to trust the old number because it was familiar, not because anyone could trace it. The work was tracing both back to source and finding the definition drift. The honest ending is not "trust the data" (preachy, and also not the lesson). It's smaller and stranger: an unexplained number that everyone agrees on is more comfortable than a correct number nobody recognises, and shipping the correct one is a social problem, not a technical one. Leave it as an observation, not advice.
**Why now:** Evergreen. Doesn't depend on any news cycle.
**Format:** Plain text. Possibly one screenshot of the two numbers side by side with labels stripped.
**Friction to keep in:** Include the part where he assumed he was wrong first, and how long it took to be sure he wasn't.
