## Angle 1 — The part of the assistant job the automation couldn't take
**Territory:** EA-to-operations evolution
**Hook:** "A CEO who said she'd fire anyone who didn't use AI went on the record this month saying the one person she can't replace with it is her executive assistant. My title still says executive assistant, and I automate things for a living, so I went and looked at what my own automations actually took off my plate."
**Direction:** Report the split honestly. The n8n expense workflow took the mechanical half: pulling receipts, matching, routing. What it never touched was knowing which expense was going to cause an argument and who to talk to before it did. That judgment part isn't "human warmth", it's context nobody wrote down anywhere the model could read. End on the open question, not a moral: if the un-automatable part is undocumented context, is that a permanent moat or just a documentation backlog?
**Why now:** Fortune, 5 July 2026 — "The CEO who vowed to 'fire anyone who doesn't use AI' admits that the technology can't replace her executive assistant as the role evolves."
**Format:** Plain text. No image needed; the piece is the argument.
**Friction to keep in:** He is the one automating himself, and he still doesn't know where the line lands.

## Angle 2 — Everyone automated screening, so the bottleneck moved
**Territory:** Recruiting observed from the ops/data seat
**Hook:** "88% of companies now run AI on the first pass of candidate screening. I don't screen anyone, I just build the dashboard that watches the funnel, and the thing I keep noticing is that speeding up the top didn't make hiring faster."
**Direction:** Strictly reporting from the time-in-stage view: when the screen gets cheap and fast, volume at the top goes up and the constraint slides downstream to interviewer availability and feedback turnaround, which are human calendars and nobody's KPI. Show the shape of it (where days actually accumulate) without telling recruiters what to do about it. Be explicit that this is one company's funnel, not an industry claim.
**Why now:** Mid-2026 recruiting stats (MSH / ResumeHog roundups, July 2026) put AI-assisted first-pass screening near-universal at 88%; Korn Ferry's 2026 TA trends frames the recruiter as "orchestrator" rather than executor.
**Format:** Plain text + one screenshot of a time-in-stage chart (numbers blurred/normalised).
**Friction to keep in:** He can only see this because it's on a dashboard he built, which means the metric is also his own artefact. Say that out loud.

## Angle 3 — Using AI vs shipping with AI
**Territory:** AI and automation builds
**Hook:** "Gallup says half of US employees now use AI at work. I counted how many of my own AI sessions this year actually ended with something running in production. It's a much smaller number than I expected and I'm the guy who does this all day."
**Direction:** The honest ratio. Most sessions are exploration, dead ends, or things that worked in a notebook and never got a schedule, a home, or an owner. The ones that shipped had something boring in common: they had a place to live (a repo, a cron, a URL) before they were good. Land on the limited takeaway — "shipping" is mostly a hosting-and-ownership problem, not a model problem — and leave it there.
**Why now:** Gallup (2026) — first time half of employed US adults report using AI in their role; 13% daily. Meanwhile Gartner/MIT-style reporting keeps showing the pilot-to-production gap. The gap between those two numbers is the post.
**Format:** Plain text. Optionally one number as the whole visual.
**Friction to keep in:** Give the actual count, including the failures. No rounding up.

## Angle 4 — EVERGREEN: the automation that failed silently
**Territory:** AI and automation builds (doesn't depend on the news)
**Hook:** "The worst thing an automation can do isn't break. It's keep running while quietly producing nothing, and I didn't notice for longer than I'd like to admit."
**Direction:** One specific incident from the n8n fleet or a data pipeline: the run went green, the output was stale or empty, and the downstream number looked plausible enough that nobody questioned it. What changed after: a check that the data is fresh, not just that the job exited 0. The honest cost is that every automation you add is a thing you now have to watch, and nobody counts that when they count the hours saved.
**Why now:** Evergreen. Always usable.
**Format:** Plain text + screenshot of the alert/check that now catches it.
**Friction to keep in:** Say how long it went unnoticed. That's the whole post.
