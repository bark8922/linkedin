## 1. 93% more applications, 0.5% get an offer — from the seat that watches the funnel fill
**Territory:** Recruiting observed from the inside
**Hook:** "The number going around this week is that applications are up 93% and about 0.5% of applicants end up with an offer. I'm not a recruiter, but I built the funnel view our team opens every morning, and the surge doesn't show up where you'd expect."
**Direction:** The "application flood" is the loudest recruiting thread right now — AI-written resumes and one-click apply pushing volume up while offer rates crater. Blake sits at the data layer of a recruiting company and built the candidate-funnel / time-to-hire dashboard. The post reports one concrete thing the data shows from his seat: e.g. the flood pooled at the very top of the funnel and changed nothing downstream, or that the real cost was screening load, not conversion. Reported never prescribed — he's not telling recruiters what to do about it, just what the shape of the pipeline looks like when volume doubles. Include the friction: the week the top-of-funnel count looked wrong and it turned out to be duplicate applications, not a real spike.
**Why timely:** MSH / HeroHunt / ResumeHog 2026 volume-recruiting pieces all circulating this week with the "93% surge, 0.5% offer rate" framing.
**Format:** Plain text + one cropped funnel chart. Could be a short before/after carousel if the shape is visual.

## 2. Everyone "deployed agents." Almost nobody scaled them. I'm a team of one that did.
**Territory:** AI and automation builds
**Hook:** "This week's reports keep landing on the same split: ~97% of companies deployed an AI agent, under 10% scaled one to actually deliver value. I run a fleet of automations for a company and I'm one person, so I've been sitting with why the gap is that wide."
**Direction:** Microsoft's 2026 Work Trend Index and the WRITER adoption report are everywhere this week with the experiment-vs-scale gap. Blake's n8n fleet (expense ops, data pipelines) is a rare case of automations that actually run in production and get depended on, built by one person with no committee. The post reports what he thinks the gap actually is from where he sits: not the tech, but that scaling means owning the boring parts — the error handling, the "what happens at 2am", the fact that someone has to notice when it breaks. Keep the friction: the thing he built that he'd call "deployed but not scaled" because he still doesn't fully trust it. Ends on the honest open question of whether a one-person operation can ever really "scale" an agent or just keep a lot of plates spinning.
**Why timely:** Microsoft 2026 Work Trend Index + WRITER "79% face challenges" report, both current this week.
**Format:** Plain text + one screenshot of an n8n canvas.

## 3. The EA metrics everyone's tracking — and the one I actually feel
**Territory:** EA-to-operations evolution
**Hook:** "The 2026 EA writeups all list the same KPIs now: meetings started on time, hours of exec time reclaimed, vendor spend consolidated. I've hit one of those in a way that's easy to count, and it still feels like the least interesting thing I did."
**Direction:** A cluster of "the EA role in 2026" pieces this week frame the job as measurable and strategic — tracking reclaimed hours and consolidated vendor spend. Blake genuinely did the vendor-consolidation thing (killed a ~$6K/month analyst + BI/pipeline stack), so the headline metric is real and countable. The post reports the mismatch: the number that's easy to put on a slide (dollars saved) is not the change he actually notices day to day, which is that people now ask the dashboard questions they used to ask a person. Reported not preached — he's not telling other EAs how to measure themselves, just noticing which metric is legible vs. which one matters. Ends on a limited takeaway about the countable win being the shallow one.
**Why timely:** Cluster of 2026 EA-evolution pieces (Zapier, Unite.AI, mean.ceo) circulating this week on EAs becoming ops/chief-of-staff and tracking hard KPIs.
**Format:** Plain text, no image needed. Stands alone.

## 4. EVERGREEN — the transform that broke silently
**Territory:** AI and automation builds
**Hook:** "The scariest thing I've built isn't the dashboard that replaced a data analyst. It's the day one of its transforms failed without erroring, the numbers stayed plausible, and nobody noticed for a bit."
**Direction:** Doesn't depend on any news. Blake's dashboards run on a pipeline (Bubble/Ashby extract → DuckDB transforms → JSON → hosted view). The post reports a specific failure mode nobody warns you about when you replace expensive tooling with something you own: expensive tools fail loudly, your own quiet script fails silently and still returns a number that looks right. Include the real friction — how he caught it, and what he added afterward (a sanity check, a row-count guard, whatever the honest version is). Ends on a limited, earned takeaway: the value of the old $6K stack wasn't the dashboards, it was that someone else was paid to notice when they lied. Now that someone is him.
**Format:** Plain text + one screenshot, or a short PDF carousel of old stack vs. new + the guardrail he added.
