# LinkedIn post angles — 2026-07-10

Five angles today. Four tied to this week's threads, one evergreen from your own work. Each is an angle, not a finished post. Filtered through the voice rules: lead with a specific thing, report don't preach, include the friction, end honest.

---

## 1. The gap between "we tried an agent" and "an agent actually runs this"

**Territory:** AI and automation builds

**Hook line:**
> Gartner says 80% of new enterprise apps now ship with an AI agent in them. Also: fewer than 10% of companies have gotten one to actually deliver value. Both numbers are true and I think I know why.

**Direction:** Report on the distance between a demo agent and a production one, from your n8n workflows. The intelligence was never the hard part. The hard part was giving it reliable, secure access to the real systems and trusting it enough to leave it unattended. Talk about a specific workflow (expense ops or a data pipeline) where the first version worked in a demo and then broke on the messy real inputs, and what closed that gap. Land on: the moat isn't the model, it's the plumbing and the trust.

**Why timely:** Two widely-cited stats this week — Gartner's "80% of Q1 2026 apps embed an agent" vs. "fewer than 10% scaled to value" (First Page Sage / LangChain State of Agents, S&P/McKinsey "31% have one in production"). The experimentation-to-production gap is the debate of the moment.

**Format:** Plain text + one screenshot of a real workflow graph (messy is fine, messy is the point).

## 2. AI was supposed to replace the assistant. It promoted me instead.

**Territory:** The EA-to-operations evolution

**Hook line:**
> A headline went around this month: "AI was supposed to replace executive assistants. It promoted them instead." I'm the guy that happened to, so let me tell you what it actually looked like day to day.

**Direction:** Report the mechanics of the promotion, not the sentiment. When the calendar-and-inbox work got automatable, the job didn't disappear, it moved up the stack: you went from doing the admin to building the systems that do the admin for the whole team. Name the concrete jump — from scheduling to standing up a dashboard the recruiting team opens every morning. Include the friction: nobody handed you the title, the role reshaped itself before anyone had a name for it, and that's genuinely awkward to explain in a job description.

**Why timely:** Fortune ran "AI was supposed to replace executive assistants. It promoted them instead" (June 22, 2026), and a wave of "EA role in 2026" pieces are framing the shift from task support to workflow ownership. You are the literal case study.

**Format:** Long-form (this one earns the length — it's a personal arc, not a tip).

## 3. Only 26% of candidates trust AI to judge them. From the data seat, I get it.

**Territory:** Recruiting observed from the inside

**Hook line:**
> A stat from this year's TA reports stuck with me: only 26% of applicants trust AI to evaluate them fairly. I don't hire anyone. I just build the dashboards the recruiters read. And from that seat, the number makes sense.

**Direction:** Report, don't prescribe. You sit on the data side of a recruiting company, so you see what the funnel numbers do and don't capture. Observe honestly that a dashboard makes a pipeline legible but also flattens people into stages and conversion rates, and that the humans using it still override it constantly — which is the tell that the trust gap is rational. No lecture on how hiring "should" work. Just: here's what the data view shows and here's what it quietly leaves out.

**Why timely:** 2026 TA trend reports (Korn Ferry, Deloitte, Gartner, MSH) all lead on the human-AI partnership and the candidate-trust problem — "only 26% trust AI to evaluate them fairly," transparency as table stakes. Fresh and widely shared this week.

**Format:** Plain text. Maybe one anonymized screenshot of a funnel view with the caption doing the work.

## 4. The "shadow IT" everyone's warning about in 2026 is just me on a Tuesday

**Territory:** AI and automation builds

**Hook line:**
> There's a new worry making the rounds: 60% of people building internal tools now do it outside any IT oversight. I read that and realized they're describing my normal Tuesday.

**Direction:** Report your own position honestly inside a debate that's usually framed as a risk. You've replaced real tooling — a ~$6K/month external analyst plus a BI-and-warehouse stack — with things you built yourself, and nobody signed off on an architecture doc. Own both sides: the speed is real (a thing that took engineers weeks now takes an ops person a day or two), and so is the governance question (who maintains this when I'm out?). Don't resolve it into a lesson. End on the open question you actually don't have a clean answer to.

**Why timely:** Retool's 2026 Build vs. Buy report (covered by Newsweek this week) — "35% of teams already replaced a SaaS tool, 78% plan to build more, 60% built tools outside IT oversight." The build-vs-buy / shadow-AI thread is hot right now.

**Format:** Plain text + screenshot, or a short PDF carousel if you want to show the before/after (old stack → what replaced it).

## 5. EVERGREEN — What €96K/year of saved tooling actually looked like to build

**Territory:** AI and automation builds

**Hook line:**
> The recruiting dashboard I built replaced about €96K/year of external tooling. The number gets the attention. The part nobody asks about is the three weeks the data was quietly wrong.

**Direction:** Doesn't depend on the news, usable any day. Lead with the headline saving, then immediately spend the post on the unglamorous middle: reconciling stages out of Bubble, a transform that looked right and wasn't, the moment you realized a metric leadership had been reading was off. Report the fix and what you'd do differently. The friction IS the post. End on a limited takeaway — something like "the savings were real; so was the stretch where I wouldn't have bet money on my own numbers."

**Why timely:** Evergreen. Pull it out any day the news angles are thin.

**Format:** Plain text + screenshot, or long-form if the debugging story has legs.
